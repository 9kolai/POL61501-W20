Syllabus

  1. Introduction: What is political participation? And why is it important?
  2. Traditional political participation: In decline or changing?
  3. The Internet’s impact on political participation: Some theoretical models.
  4. Expansion of the participation repertoire online.
  5. Slacktivism? The impact of online participation.
  6. Networked, connective action
  7. Invited and claimed spaces for participation: bottom-up vs. top-down
  8. Democratic innovations: E-democracy
  9. Democratic innovations: Direct democracy
  10. Democratic Innovations: Participatory budgeting and mini publics
  11. The participation gap: Does online participation channels increase political inequality?
  12. Revision, questions, and feedback

Assessment

Two parts:

  • Presentation and facilitation of discussion (20%)
    • Present topic based on assigned text(s).
    • Written hand-out (1-2 pages)
    • Lead a discussion.
  • Research Paper (80%)
    • Develop a more complete understanding of specific topic investigated during the seminar.
    • 13-15 pages (excl. figures, tables, and references)
    • Concise and workable research question.
    • Independent literature review.
    • Critical discussion of your topic (must be analytical and not just descriptive!)

Student presentations (20%)

You will be marked on all three elements together: the presentation, your written hand-out, and your facilitation of a discussion.

You can use these three elements to illustrate that you are able to:

  • Accurately and concisely summarise the text/topic.
  • Apply the presented theory to real-life cases.
  • Think critically about the texts/theories/topics.

Practicals:

  • 10-15min: 10min presentation max + discussion
  • Prepare at least a couple of questions for discussion.
  • Pick a topic and date by next weeek (16th Nov, 16:30)

    • Research paper (80%)

    Can take many forms, but should include the following elements:

    • Title page: Spend a bit of time to develop a precise and meaningful title!
    • Introduction (2-3 pages): Present a precise research question that your paper will answer. And justify why it is relevant.
    • Main part (10-12 pages): literature review, selected methods, theoretical framework, empirical evidence, evalutaion and analysis of data/evidence.
    • Conclusion (2-3 pages): Clear answer to your research question based on the main part.
    • References
