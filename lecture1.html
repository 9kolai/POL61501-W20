Syllabus

  1. Part one: weekly lectures:
    • Introduction to political systems: Types of polities and forms of government.
    • Political institutions
    • Public policy: Policy cycles, agenda setting, and advocacy coalition frameworks.
    • Public administration: Weberian bureaucracy, new public management, and principal-agent theory.
    • Alternative: Democratic participation in political decision-making.
  2. Part two: Self-guided study
    • Students prepare case studies.
  3. Part three: weekly seminars:
    • Student presentations of their case studies.
    • Discussion about application of theories.
    • Preparing for exam paper.

Assessment

Two parts:

  • Presentation and facilitation of discussion (50%)
    • Present a case study of your own choice.
    • Apply theory from lectures in first part of module.
    • Lead a discussion.
  • Research Paper (50%)
    • Develop a more complete understanding of specific topic investigated during the presentation.
    • 13-15 pages (excl. figures, tables, and references)
    • Concise and workable research question.
    • Independent literature review.
    • Critical discussion of your topic (must be analytical and not just descriptive!)

Student presentations (50%)

You will be marked on both your presentation and subsequent facilitation of a discussion.

You can use both of these elements to illustrate that you are able to:

  • Accurately and concisely summarise relevant parts of a case.
  • Apply the theory from the module to real-life cases.
  • Think critically about the texts/theories/topics.

Practicals:

  • 30min: 20min presentation + discussion
  • Prepare at least a couple of questions for discussion.
  • Pick a topic and date by Christmas.

Research paper (50%)

Can take many forms, but should include the following elements:

  • Title page: Spend a bit of time to develop a precise and meaningful title!
  • Introduction (2-3 pages): Present a precise research question that your paper will answer. And justify why it is relevant.
  • Main part (10-12 pages): literature review, (selected methods), theoretical framework, empirical evidence, evalutaion and analysis of data/evidence.
  • Conclusion (2-3 pages): Clear answer to your research question based on the main part.
  • References
  • (Appendices - but do not overdo this!)

Hand-in deadline: 15th March 2021 through Moodle.

Political Systems: Types of policies and forms of government

A few words about methodology

A comparative approach.

  • Contrary to particularities of specific cases.
  • Comparing similarities or differences across cases.
  • Focus on general trends and substantial subjects.

Limitations

Today focus is on Europe

  • Because we are in Europe.
  • This means that we will mainly focus on democracies.
  • Political decision-making in democracies.

What is a democracy?

Big question with no easy answer.

However, the word simply means rule (kratos) by the people (demos)

But many ways to define the people, and define how they should rule.

General trends across Europe:

Differences within Europe:

  • Written or non-written constitutions
  • Separation of powers: Legislative, executive, and judicial?
  • Executive: Presidential, parliamentarism etc.
  • Parliaments: Strong vs. weak; consensus vs. majoritarian democracies.
  • Federalism vs. unitary state.
  • Local government and regional devolution?
