Policy Cycle Theories

What is a Policy Cycle?

  • A theoretical construction!
  • Prescriptive and normative rather than descriptive and analytical.
  • Attempts to split the process of policy formation into separate stages.
  • And thus serves a basic need to organise and systematise our understanding of this process.
  • But there are many opinions about which steps this process should be split into.

Examples of (theories of) the policy cycle:

  • Laswell (1956):
    1. Intelligence
    2. Promotion
    3. Prescription
    4. Invocation
    5. Application
    6. Termination
    7. Appraisal
  • In the following decades, many scholard developed even more detailed versions with several sub-stages.
  • However, the main stages that has mostly been adopted in subsequent research are:
    1. Agenda-setting
    2. Policy formulation / analysis
    3. Decision making
    4. Implementation
    5. Evaluation

But when is a policy finished?

What does it indicate that it is a cycle?

Evolved as a critique of normative notions of ideal policy making as a linear process.

Instead the circular perspective sees policy making as a constantly evolving process.

A key part here is the notion of feedback loops: Policy implementations and evaluations create new insights that feed back into the understanding of the policy problem.

So Laswell's cyclical model has two implications for our understanding of policy making:

  • It splits policy making into separate stages that can be studied individually.
  • It lets go of the notion that policies are the result of a clear linear process.

Instead of just comparing different institutions (as we did last week), this framework allow us to focus on (specific) processes.

When does each stage begin and end?

Policy Cycle theories conceive policy making as a chronological order of processes.

But this should be understood as an ideal type, and not an accurate picture of real policy formation!

  • Policy formation is messy and can jump between stages unpredictably.
  • The different stages tend to overlap each other in real life (policies are constantly reviewed, modified, and maybe even terminated at any point in the policy cycle).
  • Policies are not developed in vacuum (Hogwood and Peters, 1983). They are influenced and influences other policies that are subsequently developed/implemented/evaluated.
  • In addition to unintended consequences (with the feedback loop of a cycle model takes into account), policies also create new problems, that can cause new policies to emerge.

So theories of policy cycles are inaccurate descriptions of policy formation.

...and for that reason does not work well as an analytical framework to explain policy outcomes.

But it allows us to focus our analysis on generic features of the policy process.

As opposed to focussing on specific actors or institutions.

And this has resulted in useful theories and general findings about each of the policy stages.

Stages of a conventional policy cycle:

  1. Agenda-setting
  2. Policy formulation / analysis
  3. Decision making
  4. Implementation
  5. Evaluation

Agenda-setting

What is the "agenda"?

Kingdon's (1995) definition:

"...the list of subjects or problems to which government officials, and people outside the government closely associated with those officials, are payinng some serious attention to at any given time."

Government (insitutional) agenda <-> media and public agenda (systemic)

And how is this agenda set?

Assumption that not all problems can recieve the same level of (or any) attention.

  • Recognition of problem
  • Take advantage of rising attention (for a variety of outside reasons)
  • Dramatising problems
  • Advancing particular definitions of problems/possible solutions.

Many actors involved, but outside control of any single actor.

Is this always true?

Key is the move from recognition of problem to formal political agenda.

So are all problems recognised on the same conditions?

Does agenda reflect "real" severety of "real" issues?

  • Outside-initiation (social actors gain public support)
  • Inside-initiation (Lobbyinng with direct access to government officials)
    • European agricultural policies.
  • Gvoernment mobilisations (top-down without relevant role for non-state actors)
    • The introduction of the Euro.
  • Consolidation (state initiation of already popular issue)
    • German unification.

Possible explanatory factors of agenda-setting:

  • Actors (who are engaging and how are they engaging?)
  • Institutions (what institutional context?)
  • Ideas
  • Material conditions
