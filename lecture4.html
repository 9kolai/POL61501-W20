The role of citizens

What role should citizens play in political decision making?

Democracy: Demos (people) + kratos (rule) = Rule by the people.

So citizens obviously ought to play some role in democracies.

So this is fundamentally a question of what democracy is.

What is democracy?

A contested concept!

In fact, Jean-paul Gagnon identifes no less than 2234 distinct descriptions of democracy.

(Gagnon, J.-P., 2018. 2,234 Descriptions of Democracy: An Update to Democracy’s Ontological Pluralism. Democratic Theory 5, 92–113.)

A minimalist definition of democracy:

People elect representatives in competitive elections to carry out their will.

Schumpeter, J.A., 1942. Capitalism, Socialism, and Democracy (inspired by Max Weber)

  • People are too ignorant and superficial to actually govern themselves.
  • Democracy as a competition between leaders (and their policies).
  • Importance of allowing leaders to rule when elected.
  • => Very limited role for citizen participation beyond elections.

Pluralist democracy and Polyarchy

Dahl, R.A., 1956. A Preface to Democratic Theory & Dahl, R.A., 1989. Democracy and its critics.

Five conditions for democracy (theoretical. According to Dahl no democracy perfectly meets all criteria):

  1. Effective participation: Adequate and equal opportunity to form preferences.
  2. Voting equality: One person, one vote
  3. Enlightnened understanding: Opportunity for all to discover knowledge about choices.
  4. Control of the agenda: Every citizens must have a say on what political matters are.
  5. Inclusiveness: Equal participation must extend to all citizens.

Polyarchies are imperfect democracies. All conditions are not met perfectly.

Citizen political engagement is more important, but citizen's key role is to elect elites.

Participatory democracy

A very broad range of theories that all stress the importance of active citizen involvement in politics in a democracy.

These views acknowledge the importance of a strong civil society to uphold a libearl democracy.

Carole Pateman is a strong proponent of participatory democracy, who argues that citizen participation in politics has two important outcomes:

  • Policies that take all citizens into account.
  • Development of social and political capacities of individuals.

Ie. participation is a good in itself, that is required to create better citizens.

Deliberative democracy

Democratic legitimacy comes from authentic deliberation, rather than voting!

The best argument should win, rather than the one with most support.

Some central authors:

  • Jürgen Habermas
  • John Rawls (and Joshua Cohen)
  • James Fishkin
  • John Dryzek
  • Jane Mansbridge
  • ...and many more

According to many, this has become the dominant notion of democracy in political theory.

What is authentic deliberation?

A lot of different notions of this, but Habermas' communicative rationality is often used as a basis. Reason giving, justification of opinions, and open-minded-ness play a role here.

Amy Gutmann and Dennis F. Thompson have developed four practical requirements for the reasons given in a deliberative democracy:

  1. Reciprocal: Acceptable reasons to free and equal persons.
  2. Accessible: Reasons must be public (and understandable)
  3. Binding: The process should lead to a binding decision.
  4. Dynamic or Provisional: An openness to change one's mind and reconsider previous decisions.

What is authentic deliberation?

Another example of concrete requirements to deliberation developed by James Fishkin and Robert Luskin:

  1. Informed and informative: Based on (reasonable) factual claims.
  2. Balanced: Arguments and counterarguments.
  3. Conscientious: Willingness to listen as well as talk respectfully.
  4. Substantive: Focus on substance of arguments and not performance.
  5. Comprehensive: All relevant points and positions should be included.

The role of citizens in deliberative democracy

  • Preferences are not determined a priori!
  • So preference formation is a central part of political engagement.
  • Process legitimacy rather than output legitimacy: Political outcomes are justified by the deliberative process rather than the quality of their outputs.
  • So the process of deliberation is crucial!
  • Different emphasis on citizen participation in deliberation.
  • Focus on inclusion of arguments, rather than inclusion of people.
  • Representativeness might be more important than quantity (according to some deliberative theorists)

Democratic innovations

Definitions

"institutions that have been specifically designed to increase and deepen citizen participation in the political decision-making process.”(p. 1)

Smith, Graham., 2009. Democratic Innovations: Designing Institutions for Citizen Participation. Cambridge University Press.

"processes or institutions that are new to a policy issue, policy role, or level of governance, and developed to reimagine and deepen the role of citizens in governance processes by increasing opportunities for participation, deliberation and influence." (p. 14)

Elstub, S., Escobar, O., 2019. Defining and typologising democratic innovations, in: Handbook of Democratic Innovation and Governance. Edward Elgar Publishing, pp. 11–31.

  1. Processes or institutions
  2. That Are New to a Policy Issue, Policy Role, or Level of Governance
  3. Developed to Reimagine and Deepen the Role of Citizens in Governance Processes
  4. By Increasing Opportunities for Participation, Deliberation and Influence
  • Theoretical concept of a real phenomona.
  • Democratically normative: citizen participation is desirable.
  • An sort of "applied political science"

A typology of democratic innovations:

Quasi-contingent features:

  • Participation selection method
  • Mode of participation
  • Mode of decision-making
  • Extent of power and authority

Contextual features:

  • Policy area
  • Level of governance
  • Stage of policy process

Families of democratic innovations:

  • Mini-publics
  • Participatory budgetting
  • Referenda and citizen initiatives
  • Collaborative government
  • Digital participation
